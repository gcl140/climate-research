YOUTH PERCEPTIONS AND KNOWLEDGE ON THE EFFECTIVENESS OF INDIVIDUAL ACTIONS IN MITIGATING CLIMATE CHANGE.

Abstract

The study aimed to assess the level of individual Youth Knowledge regarding the effectiveness of various individual climate change mitigation actions. In responding to this the quantitative data collection and analysis approach were adopted. The data were collected through an online survey of the youth 15-24 years old to respond to the gap. The data collected were analyzed and presented to respond to the study objectives. The data collected show that there is limited knowledge regarding the effectiveness of their action in climate change mitigation. But also, the data demonstrate that social media are the main source of knowledge for youth about climate change. This implies that to win youth in imparting knowledge social media is among the powerful tools to use. It was recommended to include youth to advocate for climate change and strengthen the initiative for climate change mitigation.

Introduction

Climate change is one of the most persistent global challenges, with widespread implications for environmental, social, and economic systems. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has underscored the urgency of taking swift and decisive action to limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels. The IPCC's reports suggest that even a 1.5°C increase will result in more extreme weather patterns, rising sea levels, and adverse effects on ecosystems, food security, and human health (IPCC, 2021). International agreements and commitments, such as the Paris Agreement (2015), emphasize the need for both collective and individual action. Countries are encouraged to submit nationally determined contributions (NDCs) and outline efforts to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. However, mitigating climate change requires more than government actions; individual behaviors play a critical role. Scholars and policymakers alike have advocated for increased public awareness and individual actions, including reducing carbon footprints through energy conservation, sustainable consumption, and active participation in environmental movements (Stern, 2000).


Recent global movements, such as the Fridays for Future initiative led by youth activist Greta Thunberg, have highlighted the pivotal role of youth in driving climate action. Young people, especially in developed nations, are more likely to engage in environmentally conscious behaviors and advocate for sustainable practices. However, there is growing concern that while youth are increasingly aware of the climate crisis, they may lack detailed knowledge of how individual actions translate into meaningful impact (O’Brien et al., 2018).


In Africa, the impact of climate change is particularly severe, with the continent facing heightened vulnerability due to its reliance on rain-fed agriculture, water scarcity, and limited adaptive capacity. The African Union (AU) and regional bodies like the East African Community (EAC) have developed climate change frameworks aimed at fostering regional cooperation to enhance resilience and adaptive capacity. Initiatives such as the African Adaptation Initiative (AAI) highlight the need for local engagement and the empowerment of vulnerable communities, including the youth, to tackle the climate crisis.


In Tanzania, climate change poses significant threats to livelihoods, especially in rural communities reliant on agriculture, fishing, and tourism. The government of Tanzania has committed to integrating climate change adaptation and mitigation strategies into national development plans. Tanzania’s NDC outlines goals such as reducing greenhouse gas emissions through renewable energy projects, afforestation, and sustainable land management (URT, 2021). Despite these efforts, awareness and understanding of climate change and the effectiveness of individual actions remain limited, particularly among the youth. Studies suggest that many young people in Tanzania have some knowledge of climate change but are unclear about how individual behaviors contribute to global mitigation efforts (Kangalawe & Lyimo, 2013). There is a need to assess youth knowledge to better inform policy and education strategies that promote sustainable actions at the individual level.

Statement of the problem

The global climate crisis has raised concerns about the effectiveness of individual actions in mitigating climate change, yet youth awareness and knowledge on this matter remain unclear. Literature indicates that young people are a key demographic in driving environmental change due to their openness to adopting sustainable lifestyles (O'Brien & Selboe, 2015). However, there is a knowledge gap regarding how well they understand the impact of their actions on climate change mitigation. In many developing countries, including Tanzania, efforts to educate the youth about climate change often focus on large-scale interventions, forgetting the importance of small, everyday choices such as energy conservation, recycling, and sustainable consumption (UNESCO, 2016). Without proper understanding, the youth may undervalue their role in climate action, resulting in insufficient engagement in personal behavioral changes aimed at reducing carbon emissions.

Furthermore, despite global initiatives aimed at educating the public on climate change, studies show that youth knowledge about climate change is often shallow and lacks depth, particularly in assessing how individual actions contribute to broader climate goals (Leiserowitz et al., 2020). In Tanzania, where the impacts of climate change are becoming increasingly evident, there is limited empirical research on how informed young people are about their contributions to climate resilience (Mwenda, 2019). General Objective

Generally, this research was to assess the level of individual Youth Knowledge regarding the effectiveness of various individual climate change mitigation actions.

Significance of Study

The study sought to assess the youth’s knowledge of the effectiveness of various individual-level climate change mitigation actions, highlighting both high-impact and low-impact actions. It aimed to identify gaps in youths' knowledge and analyze the factors influencing these gaps, such as educational background and access to information. Additionally, the study aimed to offer actionable recommendations for enhancing climate education programs and provide insights to guide policymakers and organizations in developing educational strategies that better support and engage youth in impactful climate mitigation efforts.

Definition of key terms

Youth

Globally, the United Nations (UN) defines youth as individuals aged between 15 and 24 years (UN, 2021). Also, the African Youth Charter categorizes youth as persons between 15 and 35 years old (African Union, 2006). In Tanzania, the National Youth Development Policy defines youth as individuals aged between 15 and 35 years, acknowledging the extended period of dependency and socio-economic challenges many young people face in the country (URT, 2007). In this study, the global view of youth was adopted as the term youth represented individuals ranging from 15-24 and was used to understand their level of awareness in action related to climate change.

Climate action

According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), climate action involves reducing greenhouse gas emissions, transitioning to sustainable energy sources, and enhancing societal adaptation to climate-related risks (IPCC, 2018). Moser (2010) emphasizes that defining climate action is not only technical solutions but also shifts in policies and societal behavior in adapting and mitigating climate crises. In this study climate action will involve also actions that have a direct or indirect impact on reducing or resolving the climate crises

Climate change mitigation

According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), mitigation involves human interventions that aim to reduce the sources or enhance the sinks of GHGs (IPCC, 2014). Stern (2007) also defines climate mitigation as the global response to reduce emissions by promoting low-carbon technologies, improving energy efficiency, and adopting renewable energy sources. In this study, the definition of IPCC we adopted as climate mitigation implies any intervention, initiatives, or actions aiming to reduce the impact related to climate change

Methodologies

The study adopted a quantitative cross-sectional research design, which was chosen to provide a detailed understanding of youth knowledge on the effectiveness of individual actions in mitigating climate change. This design facilitated the collection of data at a single point in time, allowing for a broad examination of the factors influencing climate change mitigation behavior among the youth. The approach focused on participants aged 15 to 24, residing in Dar es Salaam, Tanzania. The region was purposefully selected due to its diverse population and status as a commercial hub, which ensured access to participants from a range of social, economic, and cultural backgrounds. This diversity was essential to capture varied perspectives on climate change mitigation behaviors.


A total of 300 youths participated in the study, with the sample size determined based on the availability of potential respondents and the need for statistical validity in the analyses. The survey was administered online using Google Forms, with the link distributed through direct outreach methods to ensure broad participation. Before completing the survey, participants were required to sign an online consent form, ensuring ethical standards were maintained. For participants under 18, parental consent was mandatory before participation. The survey itself took approximately 10 minutes to complete, and responses were collected and stored using IBM SPSS Statistics Version 29 for analysis.


Data collection involved an online survey to assess youths' knowledge regarding individual-level climate change mitigation behaviors which was categorized based on their impact level on climate change mitigation. Participants were asked to rank these behaviors based on their perceived effectiveness in reducing.


The data collected were analyzed using IBM SPSS Statistics Version 29. The analyzed data were presented in tables to describe the information and its implications. The descriptive analysis was performed to analyze the demographic by calculating the frequency and percentage of the participants' demographic. But also, the Chi-squared test was performed to identify statistically significant differences in knowledge levels based on socio-demographic characteristics, and beliefs Pickering et al. (2020). Additionally, one-way ANOVA tests explored correlations between participants’ knowledge scores and their reported sources of information on climate change.

This concentration in the younger age bracket may impact the findings, particularly in studies related to issues such as education, employment, or health, as younger participants may have different perspectives and experiences compared to older counterparts.

This educational distribution may reflect the broader access to education in the community and could have implications for various socio-economic indicators, such as employment, income generation, or access to resources. The relatively low percentage of individuals with higher education may also affect the capacity for informed decision-making in areas like climate change mitigation, civic participation, or economic empowerment.

We also determined whether perceptions on effectiveness of individual level actions in mitigating climate change varied with gender, age groups, education level and area of residency. The perceptions on effectiveness of individual level actions in mitigating climate change showed no significant variation with gender (p = 0.191), age groups (p = 0.105) educational levels (p = 0.415 or area of residency (p= 0.16). We also determined if perceptions on effectiveness of individual level actions in mitigating climate change varied with personal experiences with climate-related events, Involvement in Environmental Activities, Peer and family influence. The perceptions on effectiveness of individual level actions in mitigating climate change showed significant variation with personal experiences with climate-related events (p = 0.017), Involvement in Environmental Activities (p= 0.006), and perceived risk of climate related events (p = 0.0012) but showed no significant variation with influence from peers and effect from family influence where p values were 0.46 and 0.32 respectively.


To address the second research question, participants were asked to rank six behaviors according to their perceived effectiveness in reducing greenhouse gas emissions, assigning '1' representing the highest impact and '6' the lowest. We hypothesized that these rankings would differ from those determined by a study conducted by Wynes and Nicholas (2017). Specifically, we expected respondents to overestimate the effectiveness of the less impactful behaviors. We used Wilcoxon signed rank tests to determine if there are significant differences in how youth rank the impact of various individual actions compared to established rankings by Wynes and Nicholas (2017)(5). The test showed statistically significant differences between how youth participants ranked certain behaviors compared to calculated rankings derived from Wynes and Nicholas (2017). Youths rated "having one fewer child" (Z = -13.8, p < 0.001) and "eat a plant-based diet" (Z=-7.58, p < 0.0001) as less impactful. In contrast, they perceived "recycling" (Z=-14.7, p < 0.0001) to be more impactful than the calculated rankings.


We also determined whether knowledge on the effectiveness of individual actions in mitigating climate change was associated with select independent variables. To do this we first calculated an ‘effectiveness knowledge score’ (EKS). This score was derived by taking the absolute value of the difference between a respondent’s ranking of each action and the ‘true’ rank from Wynes and Nicholas (2017), and then calculating the mean of these differences for each respondent. A score of zero indicated perfect alignment with the calculated ranks (indicating high knowledge), while a score of three indicated the greatest disparity in ranks (indicating low knowledge).


Regarding demographic factors, the effectiveness knowledge score (EKS) showed no significant variation with gender (p = 0.383) or age groups (p = 0.229). However, significant differences were observed among educational levels (p = 0.04), with participants having secondary and university education levels demonstrating lower EKS (mean 1.61 and 1.52, respectively) compared to those with no education and primary education levels, where the mean EKS was 2.3 and 1.9, respectively. This suggests that individuals with higher educational attainment possess greater knowledge of the relative effectiveness of individual climate change mitigation actions.


We also determined that the effectiveness knowledge score (EKS) showed significant variation among those with participation in different environmental activities (p= 0.015) where those who have participated in different environmental activities demonstrated lower EKS (mean 1.42) compared those who had not participated in different environmental activities (mean 1.77). This suggests that individuals with who participate in different environmental activities possess greater knowledge of the relative effectiveness of individual climate change mitigation actions. In addition, the effectiveness knowledge score (EKS) showed significant variation among those with personal experiences with climate-related events where those with personal experiences with climate-related events had demonstrated lower EKS mean 1.268 p= 0.003) compared those who had no personal experiences with climate-related events (mean 1.68 p= 0.438). This suggests that individuals with personal experiences with climate-related events possess greater knowledge of the relative effectiveness of individual climate change mitigation actions. However, the effectiveness knowledge score (EKS) showed no significant variation among among those who believed that they were at risk to climate change related events (mean 1.87 p = 0.36) and those who perceived they were at no risk (mean 1.92 p = 0.24).


We also performed a series of one-way ANOVAs tests to explore potential correlations between different sources of information about climate change, as reported by respondents and their effectiveness knowledge score (EKS). However, all tests resulted in non-significant results (p > 0.05). This suggests that no statistically significant associations were observed between any specific information source and participants' EKS in this study.


Regarding beliefs about climate change mitigation, there was no notable difference in EKS between individuals who do not believe their actions or lifestyle choices can contribute to reducing climate change (mean EKS = 1.8049; n = 251) and those who do believe (mean EKS = 1.792; n = 49 p = 0.89).

Discussion

In our study, we explored youth’s knowledge and their perception on the effectiveness of individual actions for mitigating climate change. First, we examined whether youth believe that their individual actions can help to mitigate climate change. Second, we investigated the knowledge of youth regarding the relative efficacy of different individual actions in combating climate change. Third we examined how accuracy of knowledge varied according to age groups, gender, education levels. We conducted a quantitative, cross-sectional study with 300 participants from Dar es Salaam region.



Our findings showed that a majority (83.7%) expressed belief in the effectiveness of personal actions in mitigating climate change, supporting our H1. This highlights a generally positive attitude towards individual responsibility in environmental stewardship. These findings are in line with research by Udas et al.,(2021) which has shown that youth express a felt responsibility to act on climate change and reduce anthropogenic impacts on the Earth(18). Recognizing the importance of individual empowerment and perceived influence among youth in their environmental actions, these results substantial significance.



Many participants skeptical about the impact of individual actions highlighted external factors, predominantly blaming corporations and large companies for environmental degradation. These participants felt disillusioned believing that individual actions were insignificant in comparison to industrial and systemic sources of pollution. This perspective highlights the challenge of fostering attitudes of personal responsibility, particularly given the broader environmental impacts of corporate practices. It underscores the need for targeted educational strategies and policy interventions to empower individuals and promote collective action toward sustainable environmental practices. Movements such as Friday for future founded by Greta Thunberg and other Student’s activists have mobilized many youths to action, such as through school ‘climate strikes’ (19). However, It remains to be determined if such awareness-raising will translate into a belief of greater agency amongst youth over CC mitigation at the individual level.



While the majority of youths believed in the efficacy of their actions and lifestyle choices in combating climate change, we also evaluated their knowledge around the relative impact of various behaviors. Our findings showed that behaviors with lower impact, such as recycling and reducing waste, tend to be overestimated, while the most impactful behavior of choosing to have one fewer child is underestimated, supporting our hypothesis (H2). Previous research among US adults has similarly found discrepancies in estimating the savings of different activities (Attari et al., 2010). The perceived effectiveness of recycling, highlighted in our study, could be explained by the fact that this is a behavior where youth can exert significant agency, contrasting with actions like sustainable food consumption that may require more resources and autonomy within family environments. Therefore, it is not surprising that recycling ranked high among actions perceived as impactful, possibly reinforced by frequent exposure to recycling bins and programs at school and elsewhere on a daily basis.



The most impactful behaviour rated in our study—having one fewer child—was scored as the least effective at reducing GGE. This can be attributed to several factors. Firstly, this perception may stem from a lack of understanding among youth regarding the long-term and cumulative impact of population growth on carbon emissions. Individuals may prioritize more immediate or visible actions, such as recycling or energy-saving measures, which they perceive as directly influencing environmental outcomes they can observe in their daily lives. Moreover, cultural and societal norms often emphasize personal consumption choices over demographic factors like family size when discussing climate change mitigation. Thus, conversations on climate change mitigation should broaden to include discussions on the long-term implications of demographic factors like family size on carbon emissions. This is because choosing to have one fewer child by a US family, as noted by Wynes and Nicholas (2017), can achieve emissions reductions equivalent to those made by 684 teenagers adopting lifelong comprehensive recycling habits (p3). The impact of this choice is significant due to the cumulative reduction in emissions across future generations, as highlighted in life cycle assessments by Murtaugh and Schlax (2009).



The findings of our study offer many groups of people (teachers, students, parents, policy makers or any education practitioners) with a message that while youth generally believe in the effectiveness of individual actions to mitigate climate change, there is a need for education that accurately informs them about the relative impact of different behaviors to foster more informed environmental stewardship. Therefore, prioritizing the integration of comprehensive climate change education into school curricula is essential. Such education should encompass not only the scientific aspects of climate change but also the effectiveness of diverse mitigation actions. Finally, future research could be conducted by addressing some of the limitations of the current study, by including a more geographically diverse sample, encompassing both urban and rural areas, as well as different regions within Tanzania and other countries in the global South. This approach would provide a more comprehensive understanding of youth perceptions and behaviours across various socio-economic and cultural contexts. Additionally, comparative studies between different regions and countries could highlight unique regional challenges and opportunities for effective climate change education and policy interventions. We also recommend incorporating mixed-methods approaches that combine surveys with qualitative interviews or focus groups in order to get deeper insights into the factors influencing youth perceptions and actions related to climate change.

Study Design & Participants

We conducted a community-based cross-sectional study that enrolled 300 youths aged 15 to 24 years from Dar es Salaam region in Tanzania from November 2019 to February 2020. Tanzania, an East African country, is divided into 31 regions, with Dar es Salaam serving as its commercial capital. Dar es Salaam region was purposely selected due to its diverse population and accessibility, making it representative of urban youth in the region. Study participants were recruited from their homes through door-to-door visits.

Data Collection Procedures

Data collection involved the use of interviewer-administered questionnaires. The interviewers explained the purpose of the study to interested youths and obtained their consent. For participants under 18 years old, consent was obtained from both the youths and their parents or guardians. The survey took approximately 10 minutes to complete.



The primary data collection instrument was a structured questionnaire. Participants were initially asked, "Do you believe that your activities or lifestyle choices can help to lessen climate change?" with a yes or no response option. Those who responded "no" were then asked, "Why do you believe that your activities or lifestyle choices cannot help to lessen climate change?" (open-ended response, no word limit). These qualitative responses were subsequently compiled and summarized.



To assess knowledge of individual-level climate change mitigation behaviors, participants were asked to rank six behaviors by their perceived ability to reduce greenhouse gas emissions (GGE): live car-free, have one fewer child, eat a plant-based diet, switch from an electric car to car-free, recycle, and upgrade lightbulbs. These behaviors represent four high-impact, one moderate-impact, and one low-impact actions, as calculated by Wynes and Nicholas (2017). The order of presentation of behaviors was randomized, with a rank of one indicating the most reduction in emissions and six the least reduction.



Basic demographic variables were also collected, including age, gender, level of education, and type of curriculum. Sources of climate change information were determined using a check-all-that-apply format with 11 response options.

Data Analysis

Once responses were collected, all information was coded and entered into SPSS version 24 for statistical analysis. Summary statistics were used to describe the demographic characteristics of the participants. Chi-squared tests were conducted to determine if responder status (yes or no) was associated with demographic variables. The Wilcoxon signed-rank test was used to compare the rankings of GGE-reducing behaviors provided by participants with those calculated by Wynes and Nicholas (2017).
