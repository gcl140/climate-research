YOUTH PERCEPTIONS AND KNOWLEDGE ON THE EFFECTIVENESS OF INDIVIDUAL ACTIONS IN MITIGATING CLIMATE CHANGE.

Abstract

The study aimed to assess the level of individual Youth Knowledge regarding the effectiveness of various individual climate change mitigation actions. In responding to this the quantitative data collection and analysis approach were adopted. The data were collected through an online survey of the youth 15-24 years old to respond to the gap. The data collected were analyzed and presented to respond to the study objectives. The data collected show that there is limited knowledge regarding the effectiveness of their action in climate change mitigation. But also, the data demonstrate that social media are the main source of knowledge for youth about climate change. This implies that to win youth in imparting knowledge social media is among the powerful tools to use. It was recommended to include youth to advocate for climate change and strengthen the initiative for climate change mitigation.

Introduction

Climate change is one of the most persistent global challenges, with widespread implications for environmental, social, and economic systems. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has underscored the urgency of taking swift and decisive action to limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels. The IPCC's reports suggest that even a 1.5°C increase will result in more extreme weather patterns, rising sea levels, and adverse effects on ecosystems, food security, and human health (IPCC, 2021). International agreements and commitments, such as the Paris Agreement (2015), emphasize the need for both collective and individual action. Countries are encouraged to submit nationally determined contributions (NDCs) and outline efforts to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. However, mitigating climate change requires more than government actions; individual behaviors play a critical role. Scholars and policymakers alike have advocated for increased public awareness and individual actions, including reducing carbon footprints through energy conservation, sustainable consumption, and active participation in environmental movements (Stern, 2000).


Recent global movements, such as the Fridays for Future initiative led by youth activist Greta Thunberg, have highlighted the pivotal role of youth in driving climate action. Young people, especially in developed nations, are more likely to engage in environmentally conscious behaviors and advocate for sustainable practices. However, there is growing concern that while youth are increasingly aware of the climate crisis, they may lack detailed knowledge of how individual actions translate into meaningful impact (O’Brien et al., 2018).

In Africa, the impact of climate change is particularly severe, with the continent facing heightened vulnerability due to its reliance on rain-fed agriculture, water scarcity, and limited adaptive capacity. The African Union (AU) and regional bodies like the East African Community (EAC) have developed climate change frameworks aimed at fostering regional cooperation to enhance resilience and adaptive capacity. Initiatives such as the African Adaptation Initiative (AAI) highlight the need for local engagement and the empowerment of vulnerable communities, including the youth, to tackle the climate crisis.

In Tanzania, climate change poses significant threats to livelihoods, especially in rural communities reliant on agriculture, fishing, and tourism. The government of Tanzania has committed to integrating climate change adaptation and mitigation strategies into national development plans. Tanzania’s NDC outlines goals such as reducing greenhouse gas emissions through renewable energy projects, afforestation, and sustainable land management (URT, 2021). Despite these efforts, awareness and understanding of climate change and the effectiveness of individual actions remain limited, particularly among the youth. Studies suggest that many young people in Tanzania have some knowledge of climate change but are unclear about how individual behaviors contribute to global mitigation efforts (Kangalawe & Lyimo, 2013). There is a need to assess youth knowledge to better inform policy and education strategies that promote sustainable actions at the individual level.

Statement of the problem

The global climate crisis has raised concerns about the effectiveness of individual actions in mitigating climate change, yet youth awareness and knowledge on this matter remain unclear. Literature indicates that young people are a key demographic in driving environmental change due to their openness to adopting sustainable lifestyles (O'Brien & Selboe, 2015). However, there is a knowledge gap regarding how well they understand the impact of their actions on climate change mitigation. In many developing countries, including Tanzania, efforts to educate the youth about climate change often focus on large-scale interventions, forgetting the importance of small, everyday choices such as energy conservation, recycling, and sustainable consumption (UNESCO, 2016). Without proper understanding, the youth may undervalue their role in climate action, resulting in insufficient engagement in personal behavioral changes aimed at reducing carbon emissions.

Furthermore, despite global initiatives aimed at educating the public on climate change, studies show that youth knowledge about climate change is often shallow and lacks depth, particularly in assessing how individual actions contribute to broader climate goals (Leiserowitz et al., 2020). In Tanzania, where the impacts of climate change are becoming increasingly evident, there is limited empirical research on how informed young people are about their contributions to climate resilience (Mwenda, 2019). General Objective

Generally, this research was to assess the level of individual Youth Knowledge regarding the effectiveness of various individual climate change mitigation actions.

Significance of Study

The study sought to assess the youth’s knowledge of the effectiveness of various individual-level climate change mitigation actions, highlighting both high-impact and low-impact actions. It aimed to identify gaps in youths' knowledge and analyze the factors influencing these gaps, such as educational background and access to information. Additionally, the study aimed to offer actionable recommendations for enhancing climate education programs and provide insights to guide policymakers and organizations in developing educational strategies that better support and engage youth in impactful climate mitigation efforts.

Definition of key terms

Youth

Globally, the United Nations (UN) defines youth as individuals aged between 15 and 24 years (UN, 2021). Also, the African Youth Charter categorizes youth as persons between 15 and 35 years old (African Union, 2006). In Tanzania, the National Youth Development Policy defines youth as individuals aged between 15 and 35 years, acknowledging the extended period of dependency and socio-economic challenges many young people face in the country (URT, 2007). In this study, the global view of youth was adopted as the term youth represented individuals ranging from 15-24 and was used to understand their level of awareness in action related to climate change.

Climate action

According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), climate action involves reducing greenhouse gas emissions, transitioning to sustainable energy sources, and enhancing societal adaptation to climate-related risks (IPCC, 2018). Moser (2010) emphasizes that defining climate action is not only technical solutions but also shifts in policies and societal behavior in adapting and mitigating climate crises. In this study climate action will involve also actions that have a direct or indirect impact on reducing or resolving the climate crises

Climate change mitigation

According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), mitigation involves human interventions that aim to reduce the sources or enhance the sinks of GHGs (IPCC, 2014). Stern (2007) also defines climate mitigation as the global response to reduce emissions by promoting low-carbon technologies, improving energy efficiency, and adopting renewable energy sources. In this study, the definition of IPCC we adopted as climate mitigation implies any intervention, initiatives, or actions aiming to reduce the impact related to climate change

Methodologies

The study adopted a quantitative cross-sectional research design, which was chosen to provide a detailed understanding of youth knowledge on the effectiveness of individual actions in mitigating climate change. This design facilitated the collection of data at a single point in time, allowing for a broad examination of the factors influencing climate change mitigation behavior among the youth. The approach focused on participants aged 15 to 24, residing in Dar es Salaam, Tanzania. The region was purposefully selected due to its diverse population and status as a commercial hub, which ensured access to participants from a range of social, economic, and cultural backgrounds. This diversity was essential to capture varied perspectives on climate change mitigation behaviors.


A total of 300 youths participated in the study, with the sample size determined based on the availability of potential respondents and the need for statistical validity in the analyses. The survey was administered online using Google Forms, with the link distributed through direct outreach methods to ensure broad participation. Before completing the survey, participants were required to sign an online consent form, ensuring ethical standards were maintained. For participants under 18, parental consent was mandatory before participation. The survey itself took approximately 10 minutes to complete, and responses were collected and stored using IBM SPSS Statistics Version 29 for analysis.

Data collection involved an online survey to assess youths' knowledge regarding individual-level climate change mitigation behaviors which was categorized based on their impact level on climate change mitigation. Participants were asked to rank these behaviors based on their perceived effectiveness in reducing.

The data collected were analyzed using IBM SPSS Statistics Version 29. The analyzed data were presented in tables to describe the information and its implications. The descriptive analysis was performed to analyze the demographic by calculating the frequency and percentage of the participants' demographic. But also, the Chi-squared test was performed to identify statistically significant differences in knowledge levels based on socio-demographic characteristics, and beliefs Pickering et al. (2020). Additionally, one-way ANOVA tests explored correlations between participants’ knowledge scores and their reported sources of information on climate change.

Results and discussion

Demographic characteristics

The data on gender distribution shows that the majority of the sample comprises males, accounting for 63.3% (190 individuals), while females make up 36.7% (110 individuals). This indicates a significant gender difference, which could potentially influence the outcomes of the study, especially if the research focuses on topics where gender plays a critical role, such as participation in economic activities or social behaviors. The underrepresentation of females may suggest existing gender imbalances in the community or could reflect sample selection criteria.


In terms of age distribution, the sample is predominantly composed of youth aged 15-19 years, who constitute 60% of the participants (180 individuals). The remaining 40% (120 individuals) belong to the 20-24 age group. This concentration in the younger age bracket may impact the findings, particularly in studies related to issues such as education, employment, or health, as younger participants may have different perspectives and experiences compared to older counterparts.

Regarding education levels, the largest proportion of the sample, 39.3% (118 individuals), has completed primary education. Secondary education is the second most common level, with 29% (87 individuals), while a smaller percentage of the participants (8.7%, or 26 individuals) have attained university-level education. A notable 23% (69 individuals) have no formal education. This educational distribution may reflect the broader access to education in the community and could have implications for various socio-economic indicators, such as employment, income generation, or access to resources. The relatively low percentage of individuals with higher education may also affect the capacity for informed decision-making in areas like climate change mitigation, civic participation, or economic empowerment.

We asked two research questions about the accuracy of knowledge among youths regarding the effectiveness of various individual actions in mitigating climate change and on factors that influence youth’s knowledge of the effectiveness of individual actions in mitigating climate change.

From this study youths with personal experiences of climate-related events possessed significantly greater knowledge of the effectiveness of individual climate change mitigation actions compared to those without such experiences. Factors of the effectiveness of individual actions that could mitigate climate change

Regarding demographic factors, the effectiveness knowledge score (EKS) was not found to show statistically significant variation with gender (p = 0.093) or age groups (p = 0.650). However, statistically significant differences were observed among different educational levels (p = 0.015, where participants having university education levels demonstrated lower EKS (mean = 1.9) compared to those with no education, primary education levels, and secondary education (mean EKS was 2.4. 2.3 and 2.2, respectively). We also determined that the effectiveness knowledge score (EKS) showed significant variation among those with participation in different environmental activities (p= 0.015) where those who have participated in different environmental activities demonstrated lower EKS (mean 2.0) compared to those who had not participated in different environmental activities (mean 2.4).

In addition, the effectiveness knowledge score (EKS) showed significant variation among those with personal experiences with climate-related events whereas those with personal experiences with climate-related events demonstrated lower EKS (mean 2.1) compared to those who had no personal experiences with climate-related events (mean 2.3). The effectiveness knowledge score (EKS) also showed significant variation (p-value = 0.000) among those who believed that they were at risk of climate change-related events (mean 2.0) and those who perceived they were at no risk (mean 2.3). We also asked participants whether they believe individual actions or lifestyle choices can help mitigate climate change. A majority of youth (83.7%) responded that they did believe their actions or lifestyle choices could help in the mitigation of climate change. Those who didn’t believe in the effectiveness of their actions were asked to provide reasons for their disbelief. Majority attributed their skepticism to external factors such as; placing responsibility for pollution on large companies and corporations. Additionally, some expressed a feeling of helplessness to take responsibility stating reasons like "I don’t see others doing it, so why should I?" or “Changing habits is very challenging." Some respondents displayed a lack of awareness about climate change with comments such as "Climate change follows a natural cycle beyond human control" or "I don't fully understand how individual actions impact climate change". In general, responses indicated that participants could be categorized as belonging to one of two groups—those who generally perceived that their actions couldn't make a difference and finally those who lacked awareness on climate change, its causes, and mitigation actions. The study's implications underscore the need for tailored educational and awareness-raising initiatives that address the misconceptions held by different demographic groups. Despite a high belief among youth (83.7%) in the power of individual actions to mitigate climate change, many expressed skepticism due to perceived external factors or feelings of weakness. This implies that informing individuals about climate change is insufficient. This calls for effective communication strategies to empower and engage them. To overcome barriers, such as the belief that largely affects changing personnel must focus on community-driven approaches that illustrate the tangible impacts of collective individual actions. By reducing the knowledge gaps and developing a sense of agency, such efforts could enhance public understanding of climate change and promote proactive participation in mitigation strategies.

The study also highlighted the role of education in shaping accurate climate knowledge. Participants with higher levels of education, particularly those with university education, demonstrated a better understanding of which actions are highly impactful, as evidenced by lower effectiveness knowledge scores (EKS) compared to those with lower levels of education. This suggests that greater exposure to scientific knowledge especially among those with university education does provide students with a more comprehensive understanding of environmental issues. For example, Youths with higher education levels e.g. university level are more likely to encounter discussions on the impact of population dynamics, energy consumption, and sustainable practices, which may not be covered as thoroughly in primary education(Wang et al., 2022). Similar findings were obtained from a study that examined public perceptions of climate change across 119 countries, which found that education is a significant predictor of climate change knowledge and that individuals with higher levels of education were more likely to understand the causes of climate change and have a better understanding of different mitigation strategies(Feinstein & Mach, 2020).

However, it should also be pointed out that in many African countries, only 23% to 33% of children complete secondary school, and the current gross university enrollment ratio is just 9.4%(Dispatch & Richard, 2024). Thus, it is necessary to integrate comprehensive climate education across all levels of schooling. By doing so, it is possible to bridge the knowledge gap and ensure that all individuals regardless of their educational background have a basic understanding of the most effective actions they can take to combat climate change. This will thus enable youths to make more informed decisions and have a greater collective impact on reducing greenhouse gas emissions. It is also critical to not forget that almost 11% of youth in Tanzania have no formal education(Profile, 2018) and as pointed out in our study youths with no formal education were not knowledgeable on the effectiveness of these mitigative behaviors as demonstrated by their higher effectiveness knowledge score (EKS). Therefore, policymakers need to find effective ways to reach this group of youths. Isolating them because they are not in school would be a significant oversight, as they form an important part of the youth community.

Furthermore, the study pointed out that individuals who participate in different environmental activities possess greater knowledge of the relative effectiveness of individual climate change mitigation actions than their counterparts. This finding indicates that the more youth participate in activities that directly address environmental issues, the more they learn about which behaviors and actions are impactful. Our findings align with previous research by Ardoin et al. found that students who participated in outdoor environmental programs demonstrated a significant increase in environmental knowledge and pro-environmental behaviors compared to those who did not participate in such activities(Ardoin et al., 2020). This highlights the critical role that active participation plays in enhancing understanding and awareness of climate issues. This is because participation in environmental activities often involves exposure to educational materials, workshops, and discussions that increase awareness of the causes and impacts of climate change. These further stress the importance of increasing awareness through education to drive active participation at all community levels in addressing climate change. As pointed out by a study by Akrofi et al. to achieve desired climate change-related action youths should be encouraged to actively participate in climate change initiatives(Akrofi et al., 2019).


Source of knowledge about climate change

The study also aims to understand the source of knowledge for youth about climate change. The respondents were asked to select the source of knowledge. The data presents the sources of knowledge about climate change among youth, revealing distinct differences in the frequency of responses. Social media is the most significant source, with 90 respondents indicating it as their primary means of obtaining information. Following that, the internet garnered 63 responses, while mass media attracted 53. Family and friends contributed 47 responses, with the local community at 31 and government agencies lagging with only 17 as presented in the figure below. These data show that most youth rely on digital platforms, particularly social media, for information regarding climate change.

This data implies that there are importance of utilizing social media and online platforms to disseminate information on climate change effectively. Since youth are highly engaged with these sources, educational initiatives, and awareness campaigns can be more impactful if conducted through these channels. This trend also indicates a potential gap in the effectiveness of traditional information sources, such as government agencies and local communities, suggesting a need for these entities to adapt their strategies to engage youth better.



This indicates that youth lack the knowledge of the actual effectiveness of different individual climate change mitigation actions. Such a knowledge gap has also been documented in a study by Pickering et al his study involved 17-18years old Canadian youths where impact behaviors such as recycling and reducing waste were overestimated as highly impactful behaviors(Pickering et al., 2020) and from a study by Truelove and Gillis where participants consistently ranked recycling higher than scientifically supported high-impact actions(Truelove & Parks, 2012). This overestimation can be explained by the fact that recycling is one of the most visible and easily accessible actions and is more encouraged through public campaigns, community programs, and educational efforts, which tend to create a perception of them being a highly effective action. It should also be noted that actions such as recycling and upgrading light bulbs are actions that youths can exert significant control. Other actions such as; eating a plant-based diet, and living car do require more resources and autonomy within family environments(Pickering et al., 2020).

Also, youth in this study ranked having fewer children as least effective, a finding that contrasts sharply with the calculated ranks proposed by the study of Wynes et al which ranked it as the most impactful behaviors(Wynes & Nicholas, 2017). This shows that youths are not fully aware of the significant impact that population growth has on greenhouse gas emissions. According to of Wynes et al study, having one fewer child can achieve emissions reductions equivalent to those made by 684 teenagers adopting lifelong comprehensive recycling habits(Wynes & Nicholas, 2017). Similar findings were reported in a study done by Pickering et al where Canadian youth ranked having fewer than one child as the least effective action(Pickering et al., 2020). In Tanzania where the population is projected to double every 23 years the conversations on climate change mitigation should broaden to include discussions on the long-term implications of increased population growth on carbon emissions(‘TANZANIA: Economic Update’, 2021). In this study, we aimed to explore youth’s knowledge of the effectiveness of individual actions for mitigating climate change. First, we assessed the knowledge of the relative effectiveness of different individual climate mitigation actions. Second, we looked into what factors were associated with youth’s knowledge of the relative effectiveness of different individual climate mitigation actions. Our findings showed that youth did overestimate the effectiveness of lower-impact behaviors while underestimating the effectiveness of the high-impact behaviors. Also, knowledge did vary significantly among youths with different education levels, among those who engage in different environmental activities and those who do not engage in different environmental activities, those who perceive to be at risk of climate-related events, and those who don’t perceive themselves to be at risk and those who have personal experience with climate-related events such as flooding, drought and those who have no such experiences.

This was also the case in a study by Pickering et al where having different information sources had no association with knowledge of the effectiveness of individual climate change mitigation actions(Pickering et al., 2020). This can be attributed to the fact that various sources whether it’s the internet, social media, or mass media do convey similar messages or content regarding climate change mitigation, this eventually leads to a uniform level of knowledge among participants, regardless of the specific sources(Adetayo et al., 2023). It should also be pointed out that the effectiveness of information in enhancing knowledge may depend more on its quality and depth rather than the medium through which it is received(Adetayo et al., 2023). This is to say that brief news headlines or social media posts may provide awareness but lack the depth needed to significantly impact participants' knowledge. To enrich public understanding and foster innovation in climate change mitigation, it is crucial to incorporate diverse perspectives and approaches in educational and communication strategies.

Also, our study found no significant difference in the knowledge of the effectiveness of individual climate change mitigation actions between individuals who believe their actions or lifestyle choices can contribute to reducing climate change and those who do not. Similar findings were obtained from a study done by Pickering et al where knowledge showed no significant variation in youth’s beliefs on whether their actions or lifestyle choices can contribute to reducing climate change. This points out a general lack of awareness or understanding about specific actions that can effectively mitigate climate change among both groups(Salvador Costa et al., 2022). This suggests that individuals, regardless of belief, may not have access to detailed or high-quality information about what actions are most impactful. Efforts should be made to address the gap between belief systems and knowledge. This involves creating educational content that resonates with individuals' values and beliefs while providing clear, actionable information about climate change mitigation.

Furthermore, the study found that factors such as; participation in environmental activities, perceived risk of climate change-related hazards, and personal experiences with climate change-related hazards were found to be associated with youth’s knowledge of the effectiveness of individual climate mitigation actions. These findings do suggest that youth’s knowledge of the effectiveness of individual climate mitigation actions is shaped by their involvement in environmental activities, perceived risk of climate-related hazards, and personal experiences with such events. Empirical evidence supports these findings highlighting that, from different studies, engagement in environmental activities fosters hands-on learning and exposure to climate knowledge, enhancing awareness of impactful actions(Croak & Walker, 2024). Moreover, youths who perceive a high risk from climate change are more motivated to learn about mitigation strategies, reinforcing the idea that perceived personal relevance drives knowledge acquisition(Croak & Walker, 2024). Lastly, personal experiences with climate-related events, such as flooding, landslides, or droughts, further amplify this understanding by making the consequences of climate change tangible and urgent, prompting a deeper understanding of effective mitigation actions(Spence et al., 2011).


Conclusion

In conclusion, the study demonstrates that less impactful actions such as recycling and upgrading light bulbs have their significance overestimated while most impactful actions such as having fewer than one child, adopting a plant-based diet, and living car-free have their significance underestimated. These findings underscore the impact of education, active participation in environmental activities, and personal experiences with climate events in shaping accurate climate knowledge. Future research could incorporate mixed-methods approaches that combine surveys with qualitative interviews or focus groups to get deeper insights into the factors shaping youth’s knowledge of the effectiveness of individual actions in mitigating climate change.

This study however had multiple limitations such as it was done through online surveys potentially leaving out youth with no internet access that make up a significant part of our population. Future studies may involve a community-based study where all youth will be included in the study. Also, the study involved youths living in Dar es Salaam city potentially leaving out those that reside in rural areas of Tanzania thus future research could be conducted by including a more geographically diverse sample, encompassing both urban and rural areas, as well as different regions within Tanzania and other countries in the global South. This approach would provide a more comprehensive understanding of youth knowledge across various socio-economic and cultural contexts. Additionally, comparative studies between different regions and countries could highlight unique regional challenges and opportunities for effective climate change education and policy interventions.
